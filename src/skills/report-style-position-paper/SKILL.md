# Report Style — Position Paper

An argument-driven format. The report exists to argue a specific thesis and convince the reader, **not** to neutrally inform.

## When this style fits

- User asks "should we do X?" / "is X the right approach?" — they want a recommendation, not a landscape.
- User explicitly asks for a stance: "argue for X", "make the case for Y", "convince me that Z".
- Research has converged on a clear answer and the report's job is to defend it on the merits.
- A recommendation is overdue and the user wants someone to commit.

## Tone

- Persuasive but evidence-led — never bombastic.
- Take a stance early and own it. Hedging belongs around genuinely contested sub-points within the argument, not around the thesis itself.
- Steelman opposing views before refuting them — never strawman.
- Concede limits explicitly. The argument is *stronger* when its boundaries are clear.

## Section template

```markdown
# {Report Title — frame as a claim, not a question}

## Thesis

{1–3 sentences. The exact position being argued. State the claim directly, then state why the reader should care.}

## The case for {thesis}

### {Pillar 1 of the argument}

{Evidence-backed reasoning. Each pillar is a load-bearing reason the thesis holds. Cite as you go.}

### {Pillar 2}

{...}

### {Pillar N}

{...}

## Counter-arguments addressed

### {Strongest opposing view 1}

{Steelman: state the opposing view at its strongest, citing its best proponents. Then explain why it nonetheless does not overturn the thesis. If it weakens the thesis in a specific zone, concede that zone.}

### {Strongest opposing view 2}

{...}

## Implications

{What follows if the reader accepts the thesis. Concrete decisions, actions, second-order consequences. This is where the recommendation becomes operational.}

## Where the argument is weakest

{Honest limits. The conditions under which the thesis would fail. Open empirical questions whose answers could flip the recommendation. Naming these explicitly is what separates a confident argument from an overconfident one.}

## Assumptions

## References
```

## Length norm

1500–3500 words typical. Position papers should be tight — every paragraph either argues *for* the thesis or *against* a specific opposing view. Anything that does neither is fat.

## Anti-patterns

- **Hedging the thesis.** "X might be right under some conditions" is not a position. Either argue X or argue not-X. If the evidence genuinely doesn't support a stance, switch to `technical-paper` style.
- **Strawmanning.** Quoting the weakest version of an opposing view is rhetorical malpractice and a tell that the argument is fragile. The opposition's strongest form is the one that must be addressed.
- **Sandbagging the conclusion.** The thesis goes in section 1, not section 7. Build-up structure ("we'll see by the end…") doesn't fit this style.
- **False balance.** If the evidence strongly supports the thesis, don't pretend it's 50/50 to seem fair. Imbalanced evidence calls for an imbalanced argument.
- **Pure assertion.** Every load-bearing claim needs either a citation or an explicit reasoning chain.
- **Burying the recommendation.** `Implications` must convert the thesis into something the reader can do. A position paper that doesn't tell the reader what to do is half-finished.
